What is Divorce?

Divorce is the legal process of officially ending a marriage, which terminates the legal responsibilities of the spouses and allows them to remarry. This formal dissolution is granted by a court or competent authority and addresses various aspects of the marital union, including property division, child custody and support, and spousal support (alimony). The specific laws and grounds for divorce vary by country, but in most places, it requires a legal proceeding to formally end the marriage and return the spouses to a single status.

Current Laws in the PH

In the Philippines, there is no divorce, but marriages can be annulled under specific grounds listed in the Family Code, such as parental consent issues (age 18-21), unsound mind, fraud, force, intimidation, undue influence, or physical incapacity to consummate the marriage, provided the issue existed at the time of the marriage. The most common and broad ground for annulment is psychological incapacity, which requires proving a party's inability to fulfill marital obligations. An annulment case is a complex legal process that requires legal guidance and may involve expert witnesses like psychologists and character witnesses

Why Legalize Divorce?

Divorce should be legalized in the Philippines to protect individuals in abusive or irreparable marriages, offer a simpler and cheaper alternative to annulment, provide legal options for citizens regardless of their religious beliefs, and promote individual autonomy and a fresh start in life. While the Catholic Church opposes divorce, legalizing it would recognize the reality of failed marriages and align with the Philippines' secular governance, allowing a more dignified and efficient resolution for unhappy couples.

International Comparison

Other predominantly Catholic countries such as Spain, Italy, and Portugal have divorce laws, providing that strong family values and divorce can coexist. These countries provide legal pathway to protect individals while valuing the sanctity of marriage. By studying international experience, the Philippines can learn how to craft a divorce law that respects both tradition and the realities of modern family life.
